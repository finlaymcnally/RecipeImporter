# Bound Knowledge Recovery And Detect Poisoned Workers

This ExecPlan is a living document. The sections `Progress`, `Surprises & Discoveries`, `Decision Log`, and `Outcomes & Retrospective` must be kept up to date as work proceeds.

Maintain this document in accordance with `docs/PLANS.md`.

This file is plan 4 of 6 for the March 20 knowledge-stage runtime rebuild. It assumes packet-ledger, worker-contract, and repo-owned output-authority work from the earlier companion plans, because bounded recovery only makes sense once the runtime can say what a packet is, what a worker owns, and what counts as an authoritative accepted output.

## Purpose / Big Picture

The March 20 run did not just have bad outputs. It had a low-yield recovery storm. Repair and retry were acting like a second runtime rather than like narrow bounded follow-up tools. After this change, packet candidates should be validated immediately, narrow mechanical failures should salvage locally, broad junk should fail closed, poisoned workers should be detected early, and repair or retry work should stay within explicit budgets. Stale child work must stop mattering once the parent packet or shard is terminal.

This plan is where the system becomes honest about what is worth rescuing. The right outcome is not “recover everything.” The right outcome is “recover cheap near misses, classify systemic failures quickly, and stop paying for obviously doomed follow-up work.”

The observable proof is that replay fixtures which formerly implied repair storms now produce a small, explainable number of follow-up attempts with explicit terminal reasons and no lingering `running` child states after parent truth is terminal.

## Surprises & Discoveries

- Observation: the worst March 20 workers were harmful in two distinct ways.
  Evidence: some produced uniformly malformed files, while others produced uniformly low-trust or templated outputs that still looked structurally plausible.

- Observation: late transport labels can disagree with accepted packet truth.
  Evidence: a worker can end oddly after most or all packet outputs were already on disk and validated.

- Observation: “repairable” and “worth repairing” are not the same thing.
  Evidence: the historical repair storm shows that broad junk can consume many expensive attempts while adding almost no accepted coverage.

- Observation: cohort retry fixtures could be accidentally invalidated by “good” test payloads that copied nearly the full source chunk into snippet bodies.
  Evidence: once low-trust echo checks landed, those oversized snippets stopped counting as successful siblings and no longer seeded the cohort median until the fixtures were shortened.

## Outcomes & Retrospective

Implemented on 2026-03-21. The knowledge stage now validates semantic packet-result candidates immediately, normalizes them into the canonical compact packet only after repo-side acceptance, salvages trailing `EOF` and simple shell-wrapper suffix noise deterministically, rejects low-trust numeric-only or full-chunk-echo snippets, detects poisoned workers after repeated uniform failures, and bounds repair / retry through per-kind circuit-breaker decisions.

Stale retry / repair work now finalizes explicitly instead of lingering as `running`: task ledgers keep terminal reason codes, follow-up status files record skip/cancel detail, and terminal parent packets supersede older child attempts. The resulting artifact surface is much more legible for replay and postmortem work because “why did we stop?” is now a first-class field instead of an inference.

The main open risk is still overfitting the deterministic validation rules to one saved run. The implemented answer stays narrow on purpose: only concrete low-trust shapes seen in evidence are rejected, and the salvage path is deliberately mechanical rather than heuristic.

## Context and Orientation

Validation and recovery currently live mostly in `cookimport/llm/codex_farm_knowledge_orchestrator.py` and `cookimport/llm/codex_farm_knowledge_ingest.py`, with structured follow-up execution in `cookimport/llm/codex_exec_runner.py`. The March 20 run showed malformed JSON with trailing `EOF`, semantically empty outputs, stale repair calls, low-yield retry churn, and workers that were clearly wrong in a systemic way rather than on just one packet.

In this plan:

- `immediate validation` means parse, schema, and deterministic semantic checks as soon as a packet candidate appears;
- `deterministic salvage` means narrow local repair of mechanical shape problems only;
- `poisoned worker` means a worker whose outputs or behavior show a systemic failure pattern such as repeated malformed files, repeated low-trust templates, repeated boundary violations, or repeated no-result exploration;
- `repair eligibility` means the runtime’s explicit decision about whether a packet is a genuine near miss worth one structured follow-up attempt.

This plan depends on the earlier packet-ledger and output-authority work because it needs packet state, worker ownership, and repo-owned accepted output to make good recovery decisions.

## Plan of Work

### Milestone 1: Validate packet candidates immediately

As soon as a packet candidate is produced or surfaced by the worker, validate it. Run parse validation, schema validation, and deterministic semantic validation. The semantic checks should reject:

- coverage mismatches;
- semantically empty outputs;
- blanket all-keep or all-drop collapse;
- hints-only reasoning;
- copied metadata rationales;
- other templated low-trust patterns that were explicitly seen in the March 20 artifacts.

The goal is to catch bad packet candidates at the point they appear, not after a worker has written many more outputs or after a whole shard completes.

### Milestone 2: Add narrow deterministic salvage

Before any model repair is launched, attempt deterministic salvage for mechanical cases only. Acceptable salvage examples include:

- trimming a literal trailing heredoc marker such as `EOF`;
- stripping obvious shell wrapper noise after a valid JSON object;
- normalizing a parseable near-miss structure if the transformation is fully deterministic and test-backed.

Anything ambiguous should fail closed instead of guessing. This salvage path is meant to remove obvious mechanical repair work from the model-repair lane, not to become an increasingly clever heuristic parser.

### Milestone 3: Detect poisoned workers and poisoned shards

Add classification logic that looks for systemic worker failure patterns. If a worker shows uniform malformed outputs, uniform templated outputs, repeated boundary violations, repeated no-result exploration, or repeated low-trust collapse across its owned packets, classify that worker or shard as poisoned.

Once poisoned, the runtime should stop pretending each packet is an independent near miss. A poisoned worker should short-circuit into shard-level fail-closed handling or one bounded replacement path, depending on the exact failure class. The important invariant is that poisoned workers do not spawn repair storms.

### Milestone 4: Rebuild the recovery ladder

Replace the current repair and retry sprawl with one bounded ladder. Each packet should get:

- at most one local salvage attempt;
- at most one watchdog retry if the main worker died in a retryable way;
- at most one repair attempt if the packet is a genuine near miss.

Near miss means parseable packet identity, owned coverage, and a small number of validator failures that a strict repair prompt can plausibly correct. Broad junk, zero-output workers, repeated templated outputs, and malformed packets that local salvage cannot fix should fail closed without repair.

### Milestone 5: Add circuit breakers and stale-follow-up cleanup

On top of per-packet caps, add shard-level and stage-level circuit breakers. If a shard spends its small early recovery budget on the same failure class, or if the stage’s recovery acceptance rate collapses after an initial window, stop spawning new follow-up work and mark remaining bad packets terminally failed.

Then make follow-up work subordinate and supersedable. If a packet becomes terminal, all older or parallel follow-ups for that packet must be canceled or marked superseded. If a shard becomes terminal, all child follow-ups under that shard must be reaped. If the stage is interrupted, still-running follow-ups must be marked interrupted or canceled and finalized instead of being left forever `running`.

Hard timeouts and silence timeouts are mandatory here. A structured follow-up with no useful new events for minutes should not keep the stage open.

## Validation and Acceptance

This plan is complete when:

- malformed, schema-invalid, and low-trust packet candidates are rejected immediately;
- narrow mechanical failures salvage locally without another model call;
- poisoned-worker fixtures fail closed without repair storms;
- retry and repair counts stay within explicit budgets and can be stopped by circuit breakers;
- stale follow-up calls are canceled or marked superseded once parent truth is terminal;
- accepted packet truth survives late transport anomalies as telemetry rather than being erased.

The best acceptance evidence is one replay-style test that used to imply dozens of follow-up attempts and now produces only a small bounded number with explicit terminal reasons.

## Idempotence and Recovery

Deterministic salvage must stay deliberately narrow. If a transformation is ambiguous, fail closed instead of guessing. Circuit breakers and follow-up status transitions should be idempotent. Re-running a cleanup pass over already-terminal packets and follow-ups should not reopen work or double-count outcomes.

If the first poisoned-worker classifier is too narrow, extend it using concrete evidence and targeted tests. Do not replace it with opaque heuristic scoring that future readers cannot audit.

## Artifacts and Notes
